Ultimate Pre-Purchase Checklist for Refurbished Smartphones

"Refurbished" and "tested" are claims a seller makes about a phone, not a guarantee. Here's the checklist to verify it yourself, in the time it takes to unbox it.

📅 Published Jul 18, 2026 ⏱️ 8 min read 🏷️ Refurbished

Refurbished phones are one of the better deals in tech — a properly restored phone at a fraction of the price of new. They're also sold on trust: you're relying on a seller's grading and testing process, which varies wildly from one retailer to the next. This checklist takes the guesswork out of that trust, in whatever return window your purchase gives you.

What "refurbished" actually means (and what it doesn't guarantee)

A refurbished phone has typically been inspected, had any faulty parts replaced, cleaned, and reset to factory settings before resale. What varies enormously is how thorough that process actually was. A large, reputable retailer's refurbishment program and a small marketplace seller's "refurbished" listing can mean very different levels of scrutiny, even though both use the same word.

Understanding refurbished grading

Most sellers grade refurbished phones on a letter scale, but the scale itself isn't standardized industry-wide:

Common gradeWhat it usually means
Grade A / "Excellent"Little to no visible wear, fully functional
Grade B / "Good"Minor scuffs or scratches, fully functional
Grade C / "Fair"Noticeable cosmetic wear, functionally tested

Notice that every grade above is a claim about cosmetic condition, not hardware performance — a Grade A phone can still have a mediocre battery or a partially failed sensor and technically match its cosmetic description. This is exactly why the checklist below matters regardless of the grade you're buying.

Why you should still test it yourself

A "tested and certified" label describes a process you didn't see, performed by someone with an incentive to sell the phone. That's not an accusation of dishonesty — it's just a reason to spend ten minutes confirming things yourself while you're still inside the return window, rather than three months later when you're not.

The pre-purchase checklist

  • Camera focuses clearly at both close and far range, and the flash fires
  • Touch screen responds evenly across every edge and corner
  • No dead pixels or backlight bleed on a solid gray or black screen
  • Battery health is reported clearly and matches what the listing claimed
  • Microphone records clearly, without muffling or static
  • Speaker plays cleanly at full volume, no rattling
  • Motion sensors respond smoothly when you tilt and rotate the phone
  • Vibration motor fires with a clear, consistent pulse
  • Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and GPS all connect without issue
  • Charging port holds a cable snugly, and all buttons register a press

Return windows and warranty tips

Test everything on this list before the return window closes — not after. Note the exact warranty length and what it covers in writing before you buy; some refurbished warranties cover mechanical failure but explicitly exclude battery degradation, which is worth knowing upfront rather than discovering during a claim.

Red flags in refurbished listings

  • No mention of a specific grading system or testing process
  • A price that's dramatically lower than every other refurbished listing for the same model
  • No return window, or a return window under 14 days
  • Stock photos instead of actual photos of the specific unit
  • Vague battery health claims like "good condition" instead of an actual percentage

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between refurbished and used?
Refurbished phones have typically been inspected, repaired if needed, and tested by a seller or manufacturer before resale, usually with some warranty. A used phone sold privately comes with no such process — though "refurbished" is a claim, not a guarantee, which is exactly why it's still worth testing yourself.

Are refurbished phone grades standardized?
No. Grading scales like A, B, and C are common but not standardized across sellers — one seller's Grade B can look like another seller's Grade A. Always check the specific seller's grading definition rather than assuming it matches what you've seen elsewhere.

Does a refurbished phone come with a warranty?
Many do, typically 90 days to a year depending on the seller, but it's not universal. Confirm the exact warranty length and what it covers — some only cover mechanical failure, not battery degradation — before you buy.
