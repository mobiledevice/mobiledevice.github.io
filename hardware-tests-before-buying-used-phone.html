Every used-phone listing photo looks the same: clean screen, good angle, no cracks in sight. The problem is that photos can't show you a dead pixel that only appears on a solid color, a speaker that rattles at high volume, or a battery that's already down to 70% of its original capacity. The only way to actually know is to test the hardware yourself — and you can do that in the time it takes to meet a seller for coffee.

This guide walks through 10 tests, in the order we'd run them in person. Every one of them works straight from the phone's browser at mobiledevice.tools — no app to install, and nothing that stays on the phone after you close the tab.

Why hardware tests beat a visual check

A visual inspection tells you whether a phone looks cared for. It tells you nothing about whether the microphone cuts out, whether the gyroscope has drifted, or whether the battery reports 100% health but actually drains in four hours. Those are the issues that show up after you've already paid — and they're exactly what a two-minute browser test is built to catch before that happens.

1. Camera & flash

Open the front and rear cameras and check for soft focus, discoloration, or a flash that doesn't fire. A common used-phone issue is a rear camera that focuses fine up close but never locks focus at a distance — easy to miss with a quick selfie, obvious once you actually test it.

2. Microphone

A weak or muffled mic is one of the easiest defects to miss during a five-minute meetup, since you're rarely on a call at the time. Record a few seconds of audio and play it back — you're listening for clarity, not just whether it picks up sound at all.

3. Touch screen

Drag a finger slowly across every edge and corner of the screen, not just the center. Dead touch zones are almost always near the edges, where a previous drop or a cheap screen replacement is most likely to have caused damage.

The 2-minute checklist

If you're standing in front of the phone with limited time, here's the shortest version that still covers the issues buyers report most often:

  • Camera focuses clearly at both close and far range, flash fires
  • Touch screen responds evenly across every corner and edge
  • No dead pixels or backlight bleed on a solid gray or black screen
  • Battery health is reported above 80%
  • Speaker plays a tone clearly at full volume, no rattling
  • Charging port holds a cable snugly, no wiggle

What these tests can't catch

Browser-based tests read what the phone's software reports and what its sensors respond to — they're thorough, but they're not a teardown. A swelling battery cell, internal water damage that hasn't caused a visible fault yet, or a counterfeit part designed to report false status can still slip past a software check. For a high-value purchase, pairing this checklist with a quick physical inspection (does the phone lie flat on a table, does the back panel sit flush) covers the gap. Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to test a used phone's hardware?
Running through all 10 tests takes about 10 to 15 minutes if the phone is in front of you. Each individual test takes under a minute.

Do I need to install an app to test a used phone?
No. Every test in this guide runs in the phone's own browser, so there's nothing to download and nothing left behind after you're done.

Can these tests catch every possible problem?
They catch the most common issues buyers run into — dead pixels, unresponsive touch zones, weak batteries, faulty sensors — but internal component wear that isn't visible to software, like a swelling battery cell, still needs a physical inspection.
